Vestium: building a fashion-tech startup, up to a working prototype

Context

Vestium was a fashion-tech startup with an idea that did not exist yet: scanning clothing and personal avatars in 3D with a smartphone. This was to become a digital wardrobe where users manage their clothes, try outfits on their own avatar, find inspiration and sell items with one click. The vision behind it: a bridge between real and digital fashion.

Starting point

We started as a founding team of three and grew the team to eight people. The market backed the idea: the global second-hand market was growing three times faster than the overall fashion market, and existing wardrobe apps all worked with flat 2D photos. An app with real 3D models and avatar try-on did not exist.

My role

As Co-Founder and COO, I helped build the company operationally, from the business plan to investor outreach to conversations with fashion brands. What I learned quickly: the COO role in an early-stage startup is 80 percent outward communication, with investors, partners and talent. The remaining 20 percent is everything else.

Approach

Technically, we worked in two iterations. The first prototype used LiDAR point clouds, from which a mesh was generated and cleaned up. The results were okay but not good enough. The second approach combined the depth sensor with the camera's RGB data and delivered much better results: solid meshes with detail and high-quality textures, at a quality that could compete with elaborate multi-camera studio scans, just with a smartphone.

In parallel, we had professional branding developed, the app's UI and UX were in place, and I led conversations with established fashion brands about partnerships and drove the search for 3.3 million dollars in funding.

Outcome

The funding round did not close, and in 2022 we shut the project down. The prototype proved that the technology works. What it really came down to: we lacked the product experience back then that we have today. We wanted the app to do everything from day one. Scanning, avatar, marketplace, social media, all at once. We did not prioritize.

What I take away

The right path would have been to focus on one thing, launch the app with it, collect feedback and start small. That would have required far less investment, and with real user numbers and feedback in hand, the funding conversation would have been a different one. I learned this lesson the expensive way, and it is baked into every prioritization decision I make as a project and product manager today: the core first, the rest later.
