ARD multi-platform service: giving a directionless project its purpose back

Context

The multi-platform service supports the ARD player and solves playback problems across platforms, including HLS and DASH issues, a Safari subtitle bug on iOS devices and playback problems on TV devices. Because subtitles were not possible directly in the player on iOS devices, they ran entirely through this service. Traffic was accordingly in the millions of requests per day.

Starting point

When I took over the service, the project had been running for a long time but without a clear use case. Part of the originally planned scope had since been covered by other products, yet stakeholders did not want to cancel the project. The only developer on the service did not know what to work on. At the same time, the service was anything but non-critical: a failure would have meant the ARD player showing no picture or black screens.

My role

As Product Owner, I analyzed the status quo, gave the service a clear direction again and was responsible for the implementation.

Approach

First, I contacted the stakeholders who actually benefit from the service to understand where the real needs lie. In joint workshops, we went through the old use cases one by one and assessed what was still relevant, what had long been covered by other products and what could be dropped. In the same workshops, we collected new use cases. Together with the developer and the stakeholders of adjacent products, I assessed the open playback problems and turned them into a clear plan.

Because so much traffic ran through the service, safeguarding it was just as important as new features: a high-availability and failover concept against total outages, roll-outs with fallback behavior, monitoring and rollback options. And because all the knowledge sat with a single person, I significantly expanded the documentation.

Outcome

The plan with the new use cases was implemented. A project without direction became a service with a clear purpose, a safeguarded operation and knowledge that no longer depends on a single person.

What I take away

Not every important project looks important. Sometimes an inconspicuous side service turns out to be a piece of infrastructure carrying millions of requests. The first step then is not a new feature but the question: what is this actually here for?
